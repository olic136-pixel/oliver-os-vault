# 12-04 Planning Session: Blind Trust Structuring, Jurisdiction Selection, and Trustee Governance

## Summary
## Trust Structuring Overview and Objectives
- Objective: Consolidate a client’s globally dispersed properties, businesses, and assets (held via nominees) into a structured vehicle to ensure orderly management and beneficiary access if the principal becomes incapacitated or dies.
- Target structure: Blind trust with clear separation between principal (settlor), trustees (decision-makers), nominated trustees (additional oversight layer), and beneficiaries (receive dividends/distributions only).
- Risk and control balance: Trustees have full control and responsibility over asset management and dispositions; beneficiaries have no control and only receive distributions.

## Parties, Roles, and Control
- Principal (settlor):
  - Initiates and funds the trust with assets (property, business equity, financial assets).
  - Seeks consolidation and continuity planning.
- Trustees:
  - Professional asset managers or trust firms; hold ultimate responsibility and legal authority to manage, invest, acquire, and sell assets.
  - Two trustees are recommended as a practical baseline; more can be added but excessive numbers may appear suspicious.
- Nominated Trustees (intermediate oversight layer):
  - Provide an additional protective layer; can be designated individuals adding oversight without ultimate responsibility.
- Beneficiaries:
  - Receive dividend/distribution streams; no governance or control.
  - Can invest distributions independently without conflict (e.g., sports investments).
- PEPs and high-profile beneficiaries:
  - Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs), athletes, and similar profiles benefit from blind trust structures (enhanced separation, compliance).
  - Blind trust ensures beneficiaries (e.g., minors, future presidents) have no knowledge or control over underlying assets.

## Jurisdiction, Law, and Tax
- Governing law:
  - Law applicable to trustees is tied to where the trustees are situated.
  - The jurisdiction where the trust is established (e.g., Dubai, UK, Switzerland, BVI, Cayman) sets the trust law framework, but trustee location/law is decisive for trustee obligations.
- Beneficiaries and tax:
  - Beneficiaries pay tax per their residency status (e.g., non-domiciled rules), with no upstream responsibility for trust operations.
- Privacy and reporting:
  - UK: Beneficiaries may receive asset lists annually.
  - Switzerland: Beneficiaries typically receive performance/earnings statements only, not asset-level detail.
  - Disclosure varies by jurisdiction; blind trusts emphasize privacy and limited beneficiary knowledge.

## Cost, Setup Locations, and Timing
- UK setup:
  - Filing/documentation length: ~29 pages.
  - Setup cost: “about 12” (context suggests relatively straightforward; specific GBP amount not clearly stated).
  - Timeline: Less than 8 months to establish.
- UAE/Dubai:
  - Filing cost example: ~5,000 dirhams (pegged to USD); historical context from 2010; present specifics may have evolved.
  - Historical note: Early Dubai documentation was minimal (~4 pages); current requirements likely more complex.
- BVI/Cayman:
  - Setup costs: ~$11,000 USD (one jurisdiction) and ~$8,000 USD (another), approx. $20,000 USD combined estimate depending on route.
  - Ongoing management costs can be significant; professional trustee services add recurring fees.
- Switzerland:
  - Previously used as a mail-out or consolidation locus; post-US enforcement changes impacted confidentiality practices.

## Structural Mechanics and Operational Rules
- Blind trust characteristics:
  - Beneficiaries have no knowledge or control over trust assets or management decisions.
  - Trustees operate independently within the mandate; beneficiaries receive distributions (e.g., dividends).
- Decision-making and asset transactions:
  - Trustees can buy, sell, and acquire assets and businesses without beneficiary knowledge, subject to the trust terms.
  - Authority must be specified in the trust deed; blind trusts typically grant broad powers to trustees consistent with the blind purpose.
- Double-layer trustee design:
  - Trustees (ultimate responsibility) plus nominated trustees (additional oversight) provide “double protection.”
  - Trustees cannot “run away” with funds (assets are ring-fenced in trust) but can mismanage if not qualified; professional selection is critical.

## Practical Examples and Constraints
- Example profiles:
  - PEPs and footballers receiving dividend flows without asset involvement.
  - Minors (e.g., 16-year-old beneficiaries) or high-profile individuals (e.g., future presidents) kept uninformed under blind trust rules.
- Conflict-of-interest mitigation:
  - Beneficiaries investing distributions into unrelated ventures (e.g., sports clubs) remains permissible due to lack of asset control.
- Professional trustees:
  - Firms exist to manage trusts; employing experienced money managers is standard.
  - Two Swiss clients (tennis-related) structured similarly to mitigate risks (e.g., second divorce scenarios).

- Document length:
  - UK: ~29 pages; Dubai (historical): ~4 pages.
- Timing:
  - UK setup timeline: under 8 months.
- Reporting variation:
  - UK likely provides asset lists; Switzerland provides aggregate earnings statements only.

  - Explicit trustee authorities for acquisitions/disposals.
  - Blind trust provisions limiting beneficiary knowledge/control.
  - Reporting cadence and content per jurisdiction.
- Select and vet professional trustees and potentially nominated trustees:
  - Determine optimal number (e.g., minimum of 2 trustees), credentials, and fiduciary track record.
- Tax advisory:
  - Assess beneficiary tax status (non-dom rules and local implications).
  - Cross-border compliance for principal, trustees, and beneficiaries.

## Risks and Mitigations
- Mismanagement risk:
  - Mitigation: Engage experienced professional trustees; add a nominated trustee layer; establish clear deed governance.
- Jurisdictional mismatch:
  - Mitigation: Align trustee law with trust domicile; obtain legal opinions in each jurisdiction.
- Privacy vs. transparency needs:
  - Mitigation: Choose a jurisdiction with the desired reporting standards; encode reporting expectations in the trust deed where permitted.

## Process Outline (Step-by-Step)
- Step 1: Determine objectives and blind trust necessity (PEP/minor/high-profile beneficiary considerations).
- Step 2: Select jurisdiction for trust establishment and trustee location.
- Step 3: Engage a lawyer to prepare filings and the trust deed (not an accountant).
- Step 4: Appoint professional trustees; decide on nominated trustees for additional oversight.
- Step 5: File trust documents; transfer assets into the trust.
- Step 6: Establish the investment policy and trustee authorities within trust terms.
- Step 7: Set reporting cadence appropriate to the jurisdiction (asset list vs. earnings-only).
- Step 8: Implement distribution mechanisms for beneficiaries (dividend streams).
- Step 9: Ongoing management and compliance (tax filings, audits, trustee performance reviews).
