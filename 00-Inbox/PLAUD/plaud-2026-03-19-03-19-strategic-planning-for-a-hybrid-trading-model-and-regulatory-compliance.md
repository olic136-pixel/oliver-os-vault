# Strategic Planning for a Hybrid Trading Model and Regulatory Compliance

![PLAUD NOTE](permanent/b081929870337fcf9ac1400ba3af82ee/summary_poster/card_20260409063222-v2@85d7edfed5cbdfd9e52159-1_20260409063433_125b4e00.png)


This strategic discussion focuses on designing a compliant and profitable trading platform model. The core challenge is reconciling the desire to operate a B-book (principal trading) with the regulatory constraints of a Category 3 license (matched principal trading) in ADGM. The speakers devise an innovative hybrid model as a near-term solution and outline a long-term strategy to achieve full principal trading capabilities.
------------
## The Core Regulatory Dilemma: B-Book Model vs. Licensing Constraints

This section establishes the central conflict of the conversation, detailing the discrepancy between the desired trading model and the planned regulatory framework. The speakers confirm their intention to operate a B-book model for synthetic instruments, which involves taking the other side of client trades. However, they recognize this constitutes "dealing as a principal." This operational model directly clashes with their plan to use an ADGM Category 3 license, which is strictly limited to "matched principal" trading. Operating as a full principal would necessitate a Category 2 license, a more demanding authorization with significantly higher capital requirements—approximately $2 million or more, compared to around $500,000 for a matched principal license. The challenge is not a technical one, as the technology to run a B-book already exists; rather, it is a matter of structuring and presenting the operations in a way that aligns with the regulatory permissions they can realistically obtain in the short term.

------------
## Proposing a Hybrid Model as an Interim Solution

In response to the licensing dilemma, the speakers formulate an innovative hybrid trading architecture to bridge the gap. The proposed solution involves establishing a regulated entity (Tradar) that operates under a Category 3A/3C license. This entity would feature a robust internal matching engine to net as many trades as possible, adhering to the "matched principal" rules. For any remaining unmatched "residual" risk, a separate, external, and potentially unregulated entity would be created specifically to hold and manage this B-book. This external entity's sole counterparty would be the main regulated firm, a structure they believe might fall under a regulatory exception. While the B-book revenue would reside in this external company, it remains within the group's overall "umbrella." This structure allows the group to capture the upside of principal trading while keeping the regulated entity compliant. The speakers acknowledge this is a novel approach that would require explicit regulatory sign-off, as it could be perceived as circumventing the need for a Category 2 license.

------------
## Long-Term Strategy and Phased Implementation

This section outlines the strategic, multi-phased roadmap for the platform's evolution from the interim hybrid model to a fully integrated "dream state." The immediate plan is to launch with the hybrid structure, securing Category 3A and 3C licenses with an added Virtual Asset (VA) endorsement for DeFi activities, which is a simpler notification process in ADGM. Concurrently, the company will begin the more arduous and expensive process of applying for a full Category 2 license from the CMA (Capital Markets Authority, formerly SCA). It is suggested that obtaining this license could be expedited through established connections, potentially at a cost of "between five and 10 million as fee." Once the Category 2 license is secured, the external B-book structure will be collapsed, bringing all principal trading activities in-house under the single, fully-licensed regulated entity. An alternative path discussed involves utilizing the ADGM sandbox to test the integrated B-book model within a constrained environment while the primary entity operates a simpler matched-principal model.

------------
## Regulatory Deep-Dive and Final Decision

This section covers the detailed examination of specific ADGM regulations to verify the definitions distinguishing "matched principal" from "principal" trading. Citing the Prudential - Investment, Insurance Intermediation and Banking Business Rules (PRU), specifically rule 1.3.2, they confirm that "dealing in investments as principal (not as a matched principal)" unequivocally requires a Category 2 license. They explore potential wiggle room, noting a condition where a firm is considered a matched principal if its positions are "incidental in nature," held for a brief time, and do not exceed 15% of the firm's tier one capital. However, they conclude this is a subjective clause entirely at the regulator's discretion and cannot be relied upon. Based on this analysis, they finalize their immediate course of action: to concentrate efforts on securing the Category 3A and 3C licenses with a VA notification. This pragmatic approach allows them to start operations while deferring the more complex, fully integrated principal trading model for future development once the necessary licenses and capital are in place.
