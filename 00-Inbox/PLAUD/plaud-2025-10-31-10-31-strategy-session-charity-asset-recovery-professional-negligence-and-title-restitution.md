# 10-31 Strategy Session: Charity Asset Recovery, Professional Negligence, and Title Restitution

## Summary
## Case Overview and Objectives
- Objective: Recover charitable assets, pursue professional negligence and breach of duty claims, and secure damages and/or restitution for undervalue disposals and improper transfers; prioritize regaining land to enable development and maximize asset value.
- Context: Charity originally received multiple assets (school land and other plots) intended for community benefit; subsequent transactions breached lease terms and charity law, with potential criminality (money laundering) and conflicts of interest.
## Key Assets and Transactions
### School Land (Gifted 2009 by Archdiocese of Liverpool)
- Gift details: Archdiocese transferred school land to the charity in 2009; covenants held by Archdiocese were lifted upon transfer to enable development (intended for 800 homes per Archdiocese statement to the BBC).
- Lease structure:
  - Diocis granted a 150-year lease to the charity in 2009.
  - Lease term began 2009; restrictive clause: no sublease to an unconnected company (only to a subsidiary allowed).
- Immediate breach (same day as gift):
  - Brabners (representing both Archdiocese and charity) facilitated a sublease from the charity to Eldonian Group Limited (unconnected private company), breaching lease terms.
- 2017 disposal:
  - Charity received the lease back due to Eldonian Group liquidation.
  - Eldonian Group sold the remaining 149-year lease to Benares Holdings (Nevis, offshore) for £1 with title guarantee.
- Charity law compliance failures (Charities Act 2011):
  - No independent valuation.
  - No advertisement of the sale.
  - No Charity Commission consent.
  - Charity Commission conclusion: sale unlawful; likely void (or voidable); subsequent transfers invalid; asset should remain with the charity.
- Physical status:
  - School burned (arson by homeless person); insurance payout received by McGann; school demolished.
  - No restrictive covenants preventing redevelopment; charitable covenants lifted when Archdiocese transferred to charity.
### Garden Center Plot
- Current ownership: Chisholm Hale Limited (owned by Howard Gwynn, also associated with strip clubs in Liverpool).
- Transaction details:
  - Previously held by Eldonian Group (charitable subsidiary).
  - Gwynn, as director before liquidation, transferred the asset to his own company for ~£20,000 prior to liquidation (self-dealing, undervalue).
### Nursery
- Transaction details:
  - Originally charitable.
  - Sold by Eldonian Group to 7 Ruby Road Limited (British Virgin Islands) for ~£150,000.
### Canal-Side Plot (Former Garden Center and Bowling Green)
- Planning status:
  - Large plot along Leeds–Liverpool Canal; planning permission approved after appeal.
  - Current move to vary planning to add more housing.
  - Strategy contemplated: challenge planning via charitable title/void transfer arguments and attempt to recover land.
- Title fragmentation:
  - Single plot split into 11 pieces across multiple companies:
    - Entities include British Virgin Islands company, Nevis company, and Liverpool Locks (UK).
- Development potential:
  - Proximity: ~0.5 mile from Everton’s new stadium and Liverpool city center; walking distance to canal.
  - Market values: nearby new-build 2-bed houses &gt; £200,000; flats ~£120,000.
  - Conservative capacity: school land ~500+ homes; garden center plot potentially 100–200 homes; canal-side plot already has planning.
## Entities and Governance
- Structures:
  - Housing Association (irrelevant for primary strategy except as fund source implicated in transfers).
  - Charity (core owner of assets and beneficiary).
  - Eldonian Group Limited (renamed L3 Group Limited): originally 100% charitable subsidiary intended to raise money and hold assets; in liquidation since 2017.
- Eldonian Group governance changes (2002):
  - Directors included Lawrence Holden (named partner of Brabners) and senior legal counsel for Home and Bargains.
  - Articles amended to cease charitable subsidiary status while retaining assets worth ~£1.4 million (nursery, bowling green, sports center, ~£700k cash), plus land later purchased with charity money (thus argued to be held on trust for the charity).
- Liquidation status:
  - Multiple liquidators resigned under threats (e.g., cars set on fire).
  - Current liquidator identified disposals at undervalue.
## Evidence and Admissions
- Brabners involvement:
  - Represented both sides in 2009 school land transaction.
  - Internal communications: acknowledged the transfer structure as a “sham” designed to satisfy Archdiocese requirements (anticipating transfer away from the charity despite legal restrictions).
  - Longstanding involvement since 1987; senior partners intertwined with Eldonian directors.
- Mishcon de Reya:
  - Conducted money laundering checks; four failed; two referred to HMRC per internal notes; question remains whether Brabners made formal referrals.
- Documentary support:
  - Winding up petition process yielded extensive documents and admissions from McGann describing how sales bypassed legal procedures.
- Media pressure:
  - The Guardian planning to publish the “sham” narrative; court mentions bolster public scrutiny.
## Legal Theories and Claims
### Title and Restitution
- Void/voidable transfers:
  - Charities Act violations render school land sale likely void; subsequent transfers invalid.
  - Estoppel defense risk: offshore buyer may claim good faith purchase and nominal consideration; however, equity requires true good faith and compliance; outcome could still return title or yield significant compensation.
### Professional Negligence
- Targets:
  - Brabners: conflicted representation, facilitating unlawful sublease and sham structuring; failures in charity law compliance; potential AML failures.
  - Hill Dickinson: represented charity in 2017 sale of village hall; alleged failures regarding connected persons, valuation, advertising, and Charity Commission consent.
- Insurance:
  - Preference to enforce against solicitors’ professional indemnity insurance for recoverability certainty.
### Breach of Duty and Misapplication of Charitable Property
- Purported trustees and officers:
  - Breach of fiduciary duties (e.g., ss. duties akin to Charities Act and trustee obligations) in disposals and misapplication of funds.
- De facto director liability:
  - McGann identified by judge as a de facto director; exposure for decisions and transfers.
### Conflicts and Connected Persons
- 2017 Village Hall sale:
  - Charity sold massive village hall and adjoining field to Housing Association for £292,000.
  - At the time: McGann’s sister and mother were charity trustees; father chaired Housing Association—connected persons requiring Charity Commission approval, independent valuation, advertisement.
  - Same-day transfer: £90,000 “loan repayment” to Anthony’s sister (not previously in accounts), within 14 months the £292,000 was spent by a non-operational entity.
### Money Laundering and Private Prosecution Potential
- Pattern:
  - Housing Association made charitable donations to the charity; charity transferred same-day to Eldonian Leisure (subsidiary), which paid McGann family salaries; indicates misdirection of charitable funds.
- Private prosecution:
  - Considered for theft, fraud, money laundering; punitive dimensions discussed.
- Freezing/restraint orders:
  - Strategy to restrain assets (e.g., against Marie McGann’s business holdings) to prevent dissipation.
### Damages Framework
- Compensatory damages:
  - UK approach aims to restore claimant to pre-loss position; not to profit.
- Punitive/exemplary damages:
  - Potentially available where dishonesty or egregious wrongdoing warrants marking the conduct; increased damages possible in certain wrongdoing scenarios.
## Asset Recovery and Valuation Potential
- Land recovery prioritized:
  - Regaining school land and other plots yields strongest value versus damages pegged to historical undervalue (e.g., 2017 levels likely modest).
- Development valuations:
  - School land: conservative estimate suggests substantial value (e.g., 500+ homes given location and market comparables).
  - Garden center: half the size of school land; potential 100–200 homes.
  - Canal-side plot: planning already approved; incremental value with increased units.
## Defendants and Enforcement Feasibility
- Individuals:
  - McGann: owns £600k house outright; enforceable asset.
  - Marie McGann: owns 100% of a business with ~£14 million in bank; stated under oath turnover of £28 million/year; asset restraint possible.
  - Lisa Clark and other family members: implicated in transfers and loan repayment.
- Corporate:
  - Offshore entities (Nevis, BVI) holding title interests; fragmentation complicates recovery but strengthens narrative of asset shielding.
- Solicitors:
  - Brabners, Hill Dickinson, Mishcon de Reya: exposure via professional negligence and AML lapses; PI insurance offers reliable enforcement.
## Regulatory Findings and Support
- Charity Commission:
  - Concluded: transfers unlawful, not in charity’s best interests, undervalued, breached charity law.
  - Legal opinion obtained; groundwork supports void/voidable posture.
- Planning bodies:
  - Initial refusal, subsequent approval on appeal; current variation sought to add more housing; legal challenges possible based on title defects and charitable status.
## Strategic Approach
- Dual track:
  - Title recovery: pursue declarations of void transfers; seek vesting orders to return land to the charity; challenge estoppel claims.
  - Damages and negligence: sue Brabners and Hill Dickinson; include McGann and trustees for breach of duty and misapplication; leverage PI insurance.
- Litigation structure:
  - Sue McGann and force joinder of Brabners; consolidate claims across assets and transactions.
- Evidence leverage:
  - Use internal “sham” admission and AML failures; Charity Commission findings; liquidator reports; witness statements from Commission meetings; court mentions.
- Public and regulatory pressure:
  - Guardian coverage amplifies leverage; potential HMRC/AML referrals increase risk for defendants.
## Risks and Considerations
- Estoppel/good faith purchaser arguments by offshore entities.
- Enforcement against individuals may face bankruptcy tactics; hence preference for solicitor PI insurance recovery.
- Fragmented title complicates unified recovery; may require multiple proceedings across jurisdictions.
- Planning permissions may advance; necessity to act quickly to prevent further development on contested title.
## Financial Flows and Misconduct Examples
- Housing Association donations cycling to Eldonian Leisure same day; salaries to McGann family; pattern consistent with laundering/misuse.
- 2017 village hall sale to connected entity without required safeguards; immediate £90k repayment to trustee’s sister; full dissipation of £292k within 14 months by entity with no activities.
- Garden center and nursery transfers at undervalue to insider-owned or offshore companies.
## Desired Outcomes
- Return of school land and other charitable plots to the charity.
- Injunctions or restraints to prevent dissipation and further development on contested land.
- Monetary recovery for loss of use/value during dispossession period; increased damages where justified.
- Professional negligence awards against law firms; accountability for trustees and de facto directors.
