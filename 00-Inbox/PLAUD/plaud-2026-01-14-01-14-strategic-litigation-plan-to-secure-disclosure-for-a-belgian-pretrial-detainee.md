# 01-14 Strategic Litigation Plan to Secure Disclosure for a Belgian Pretrial Detainee

## Summary
# Strategic Litigation Plan to Secure Disclosure for a Belgian Pretrial Detainee

## Case Background and The Client's Predicament
The client, extradited from the UK to Belgium under an assurance that pretrial detention would be limited, has now been held in pretrial detention for more than three years. This duration is considered excessive even within Belgian practice. The matter sits within a large, high-profile prosecution—referred to as the Kiefer-Rochem case in Brussels—featuring “big guys, high value targets,” and multiple defendants whose cases risk being conflated.

The case is based in Bruges, though hearings occur in Brussels for safety reasons. A procedural crisis has emerged: after tensions between counsel and the bench, judges abruptly closed proceedings without further hearings and deferred a decision until March. Defense counsel objected and filed a recusal (revocation) request alleging lack of impartiality. This application suspends proceedings, prolonging detention. Counsel intends to file a request for the client’s release, with a new application anticipated imminently.

Comparative benchmarks were raised: in the UK, defendants at risk of pretrial detention exceeding a maximum likely sentence would be released; in the Netherlands, release would also likely have occurred despite generally higher sentences there. The client is said to face a maximum of five years, with actual custodial time potentially one-third in Belgium—leading to the view that he has “already served way above what he actually has to face.” The client derisively labeled Belgium “a monkey country,” a sentiment with which counsel partially concurred, underscoring the frustration with the current stalemate.

## Proposed Litigation Strategy and Primary Objective
The team’s central strategic move is to initiate proceedings in the UK against the prosecuting authority (referred to by default as the CPS, with awareness that another UK authority, such as the NCA, may be implicated). The driving purpose is not necessarily to win on liability but to trigger disclosure. The objective is to compel production of correspondence and assurances exchanged between UK and Belgian authorities, aiming to prove that Belgian prosecutors misinformed their UK counterparts about the anticipated length of proceedings and detention. Even if the UK authority deflects blame by disclosing Belgian-origin information, that disclosure would itself be a strategic success, providing material to challenge the Belgian prosecution or to inform subsequent applications before Belgian courts.

In short, the litigation functions as a disclosure device: success is defined by obtaining evidence of what the Belgians told the UK, which can then be leveraged either to support immediate release efforts or to attack the integrity of the assurances underpinning the extradition.

## Legal Pathway and Procedural Analysis
Counsel’s preliminary legal analysis contemplates a civil claim in the UK—potentially grounded in the Human Rights Act (with Article 8 considered more promising than Article 6 on first principles)—rather than judicial review. The choice is tactical: a standard civil claim avoids the permission filter inherent in judicial review, which could block the matter on the papers before disclosure obligations arise. The principle of mutual trust means UK authorities are ordinarily entitled to rely on facially adequate foreign assurances, so liability would require showing bad faith or knowledge that information received was false. That bar is high, reinforcing the emphasis on disclosure rather than merits victory.

Envisioned procedural sequence:
- File a civil claim to place the matter before a court and activate procedural disclosure mechanisms.
- Expect the defendant authority to apply for strike-out.
- Resist strike-out by demonstrating a prima facie case—a real, arguable claim without needing to prove ultimate success.
- If the claim survives strike-out, enter the disclosure phase and seek the critical inter-authority correspondence.
- Upon obtaining the necessary material, consider discontinuing the claim in light of realistic merits prospects.

Risks include pushback at the threshold stage and no guarantee of surviving strike-out. However, counsel expressed confidence that, with a proper legal “hook,” the claim could pass the prima facie threshold. The team also acknowledged that the CPS might not be the correct defendant, but naming CPS now serves the immediate goal of forcing clarification and disclosure; the process can then surface which UK office actually handled the matter.

## Discussion of Costs, Risks, and Timelines
Adverse costs risk arises if the claimant discontinues after forcing disclosure. That exposure is framed as “the cost of doing battle,” with the caveat that the other side’s recoverable costs must be reasonable and that more front-loaded work sits with the claimant (assembling papers, drafting) than the defendant at early stages. A recent judicial review that proceeded to a full hearing with two silks generated costs “somewhere in the region of 25,000,” though that figure is not a direct proxy here and the proceeding type differs. A reliable cost estimate at this stage is difficult.

To manage uncertainty, counsel proposed staged fees:
- Stage 1: Time to review the papers and draft the claim (the most significant upfront cost on the claimant’s side).
- Stage 2: Costs to appear in court and resist a strike-out.
- Stage 3: Subsequent stages as warranted.

This structure allows the client—who has funds but not unlimited means—to assess progress and spend incrementally. Defense counsel noted that UK extradition lawyers hold the relevant papers and can supply them promptly. On timing, disclosure is not expected within weeks; reaching the disclosure phase could take months, not years. One practitioner estimate suggested three to four months might be feasible for obtaining some disclosure, whereas a full trial would likely take at least a year, and potentially years.
